The ruling Democratic Party  is poised to unilaterally pass a controversial revision of the National Intelligence Service  Act, which could seriously impact our national security./n Given such ominous ramifications, the DP should seek a national consensus first./n The DP approved an amendment to the NIS Act on its own in a subcommittee meeting Tuesday at the National Assembly despite vehement opposition from the opposition People Power Party ./n The proposed revision includes a ban on the spy agency’s political engagements and an ending of its anticommunist activities in three years./n  The ruling party plans to submit the revision to the Legislation and Judiciary Committee immediately after going through a session of the Intelligence Committee on Friday and railroad the revised act through a full session of the National Assembly in December./n The DP proposed the revision in the previous Assembly, but it could not proceed because the Legislation and Judiciary Committee was headed by an opposition lawmaker./n After winning a landslide victory in the April 15 parliamentary elections, however, the DP has been pressing ahead with the revision./n  There has been no public hearing on the issue./n The revision raises alarms on our security front./n First of all, it did not specify what government organization will take over the NIS’s counter-communist investigations./n The ruling party wants to allow the police to take them over, but the PPP wants to keep them with the spy agency or another independent body./n If the police gets the job, the country could become a so-called “police republic” as in the past./n  Intelligence experts are expressing concerns about inefficiency when the police are left to handle both domestic and counter-communist intelligence despite the top spy agency’s relative strength in anticommunist activities./n In fact, North Korea has persistently demanded South Korea scrap any such laws claiming they are a stumbling block to unification./n In particular, Pyongyang called for the abolishment of Article 7 of the National Security Law, which mandates strict punishments for pro-North Korea activities, and the NIS’s rights to investigate counter-communist activities, as well as the pullout of the U./nS./n forces./n  Our people’s security awareness has weakened since the launch of the Moon administration in 2017./n Who
